Mars

Mars is the fourth planet in our solar system, known as the Red Planet due to the presence of iron oxide on its surface. Olympus Mons, the largest volcano in our solar system, stands at 22 km tall with a diameter of 600 km—twice the height of Mount Everest. Despite being extinct, its summit has a ringed caldera with collapsed pits and layered lava flows. Valles Marineris, often called the "Grand Canyon of Mars," is a system of canyons stretching 3000 km with a depth of 8 km. Located from west to east, it neighbors Noctis Labyrinthus and chaotic terrain, with adjacent long channels like Simud and Tiu Vallis. Mars' North Pole is characterized by a polar region mainly composed of carbon dioxide (dry ice) and frozen water, forming an ice cover during the Martian winter. Similarly, the South Pole experiences seasonal changes with CO2 and water ice, revealing a substantial deposit of frozen water beneath the surface, making it a target for future exploration. Phobos, named after the Greek god of fear, orbits Mars every 7 hours and 39 minutes, moving swiftly in its proximity. It's one of the most photographed and studied moons in the solar system. Deimos, named after the Greek god of terror, has a slower orbit, taking approximately 30.3 hours for a complete revolution around Mars. Its smaller size and greater distance make it a more challenging moon to study.
